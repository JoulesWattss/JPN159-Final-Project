SPACE INVADERS

The Invasion That Created an Industry

"I took the space theme from Space Battleship Yamato, and the game concept was inspired by Breakout. But instead of bouncing a ball to destroy bricks, you shoot at a wall of aliens—and they shoot back."
- Tomohiro Nishikado, Creator of Space Invaders

The Alien Invasion of 1978

Birth of a Genre

Released in June 1978 in Japan, Space Invaders didn't see immediate success. But by year's end, over 100,000 arcade machines had been sold. The game was so popular it caused a shortage of 100-yen coins!

Originally titled "Space Monsters," the game was renamed by Nishikado's superiors. It combined the space theme from anime with Breakout's gameplay, creating the template for all shoot 'em up games.

Simple Yet Revolutionary

The beauty of Space Invaders lies in its simplicity: slide left and right, fire one missile at a time, defend Earth from 55 aliens marching in formation. The ominous marching sound speeds up as you destroy ships, building tension with every shot.

Like all games of its era, Space Invaders is futile—you will always lose, and the aliens will always win. But how long can you last? That eternal challenge defined arcade gaming.

The First Licensing Deal

In 1980, Atari programmer Rick Maurer created a port for the Atari 2600, leading to the first official licensing of an arcade game to a home console. The home version sold over 1 million copies in its first year, turning the Atari 2600 into a massive success.

The Atari version boasted "112 video games"—actually just variations including moving shields, zigzagging missiles, and invisible invaders. This set the template for home gaming adaptations.

Cultural Phenomenon

Space Invaders became one of the most widely ported games ever, appearing on everything from LED handhelds to digital watches. It kicked off the entire shoot 'em up genre, inspiring Galaga, Galaxian, Defender, and countless others.

The game's grid of aliens, shield barriers, and bonus UFO became iconic gaming symbols. Its influence extends far beyond gaming—the pixelated alien is now a universal symbol of retro gaming culture.

Legacy & Impact

  • Created the shoot 'em up genre that dominated arcades for years
  • First game to cause a currency shortage due to popularity
  • Pioneered the arcade-to-home licensing model
  • Established high scores as the primary gaming achievement
  • Introduced increasing difficulty through speed mechanics
  • Inspired the "golden age" of arcade gaming (1978-1983)
